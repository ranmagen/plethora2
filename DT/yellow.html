Prehistoric Medicine

Nothing is known for certain about Medicine in prehistoric times. Historians' statements about prehistoric medicine are usually based on ancient archaeological discoveries.

The defining characteristic of prehistoric societies is that the people of these societies could not write. They could not therefore pass on medical knowledge beyond what could be remembered. However, many of the primitive people that survived in the modern age seemed to have built up a system of skills and behaviours that kept them healthy in their environment, despite not having anything written down. They have done this through a process of trial and error and natural selection, and it seems likely that the people of prehistoric times were similar.

Most prehistoric peoples were nomadic - so they did not settle down and build things like hospitals, neither did they have enough consistency in their own lives to observe how all human bodies work the same. Anthropologists have found primitive tribes who have not yet realised that the act of sex leads to childbirth.

The primitive technology of prehistoric people put them at the mercy of the elements, and led to a system of beliefs that saw humankind as being at the mercy of unpredictable spirits. These were said to bring life, death, health and disease. Such ideas led to a world in which spiritual rituals and the shaman, or witch-doctor, dominated medicine.
